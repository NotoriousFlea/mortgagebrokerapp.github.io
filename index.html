Preface: Introduction to Real Estate and Mortgage Brokerage

This preface sets the stage for the entire course, which is designed to provide the educational background needed to qualify for registration as a mortgage or submortgage broker under the BC *Mortgage Brokers Act*. Its primary goal is to ensure registrants have the knowledge to protect the public and all parties involved in a mortgage transaction.

Land Ownership in Canada

A core concept is that true "absolute ownership" of land in Canada rests with the Crown (i.e., the federal and provincial governments). The Crown holds the right to expropriate land (with compensation) and to regulate its use (zoning, building codes). Private individuals do not "own" land in the absolute sense; instead, they own an *interest* in the land. The greatest bundle of rights an individual can hold is a **fee simple estate**. This grants the holder the right to use, sell, lease, or will the property, subject only to the Crown's rights and other laws.

Land Value

The value of land is not derived from the soil itself, but from the *use* and *services* it provides (e.g., shelter, space for a business). The value is the amount a user is willing to pay for the right to use it. Because these benefits extend into the future, the value of an interest in land is its **capitalized value**â€”the dollar amount today that is justified by all anticipated future benefits, considering risk and current investment conditions.

Key Participants in the Mortgage Market

A typical mortgage transaction involves a complex network of professionals. The manual provides a diagram (recreated below) and details the role of each participant:

  • Borrower (Mortgagor): The individual or entity seeking funds to finance a property purchase or for other reasons (renovations, debt consolidation). The borrower *grants* the mortgage to the lender as security for the loan.
  • Lender (Mortgagee): The individual or institution (bank, credit union, trust company, private individual) that provides the funds (the loan) and *receives* the mortgage as security.
  • Mortgage Broker: An intermediary who works *for the borrower*. Their role is to explain mortgage products, provide access to various lenders (both institutional and private), shop for the best interest rates and terms, and assist with strategic planning.
  • Broker Compensation: Brokers are typically paid a commission by the lender, calculated in **basis points (bps)** where 100 bps = 1% of the loan amount. They may also earn trailer fees or renewal fees. All compensation must be disclosed.
  • Other Mortgage Originators: These are in-house employees of a specific lender (e.g., a "Mortgage Specialist" at a bank) who only sell that lender's products.
  • Appraiser: Determines the *market value* of the property. This is crucial for the lender to ensure the property is sufficient security for the loan amount.
  • Real Property Assessor: Works for BC Assessment (a Crown corporation) to determine the *assessed value* of a property for property tax purposes. This is different from market value.
  • Real Estate Representative (Licensee): Licensed under the *Real Estate Services Act (RESA)* to represent buyers and sellers in the purchase and sale of real estate.
  • Property Inspector: Hired by the buyer to conduct a comprehensive visual examination of the property's structure and systems (plumbing, electrical, roof, etc.) to identify potential defects.
  • Lawyers/Notaries Public: Essential for closing the transaction. They draft and review contracts, search the property's title, prepare closing documents, register the transfer and mortgage at the Land Title Office, and manage the flow of funds.
  • Mortgage Insurers (CMHC, Sagen, Canada Guaranty): Required for "high-ratio" loans (where the down payment is less than 20%, or the loan-to-value is over 80%). The insurance policy is paid for by the borrower but exists to protect the *lender* from loss if the borrower defaults.

Residential vs. ICandI Markets

The real estate market is broadly split into two categories:
  • Residential: Single-family homes, condos, etc. Characterized by a high volume of transactions, smaller dollar amounts, and decisions that can be influenced by emotion.
  • Industrial, Commercial and Investment (ICandI): Shopping centres, office buildings, apartment buildings. Characterized by a low volume of transactions, much larger dollar amounts, complex financing, and decisions based on financial analysis rather than emotion.

Characteristics of Real Property

Real property is a unique commodity due to four key characteristics:
  1. Immobility: Land cannot be moved. This means its value is intrinsically linked to its location, and real estate markets are local in nature.
  2. Externalities: A consequence of immobility. The value of a property is affected by what is around it (e.g., a new park increases value, a new factory decreases it).
  3. Durability: Land is indestructible, and the improvements (buildings) on it are extremely long-lasting. This means the existing stock of properties dominates the market. We must distinguish between *physical life* (how long it stands) and *economic life* (how long it is useful).
  4. Divisibility of Ownership: A single property can have its ownership divided *physically* (e.g., subdivision) or *legally* (e.g., strata properties, co-ownership, leasing).

Organization of the Industry in BC

The industry is governed by both statutory organizations (the law) and voluntary organizations (professional associations).
  • Statutory (Mandatory):
    • BCFSA (BC Financial Services Authority): The official, independent Crown agency that regulates the financial services sector, including mortgage brokers. It administers the *Mortgage Brokers Act*.
    • Registrar of Mortgage Brokers: The specific regulator within BCFSA responsible for registration, investigation, and discipline of mortgage brokers.
    • Real Estate Errors and Omissions Insurance Corporation (EandO): Provides the mandatory liability insurance that all licensees must carry to protect the public from loss due to licensee negligence.
  • Voluntary (Professional):
    • CMBA-BC (Canadian Mortgage Brokers Association - BC): The primary professional association for brokers in BC. It provides education, advocacy, and enforces a member Code of Ethics.
    • MBIBC (Mortgage Brokers Institute of BC): Provides professional education, particularly for re-licensing requirements.
    • Mortgage Professionals Canada (MPC): The national association that represents the industry, conducts research, and offers the Accredited Mortgage Professional (AMP) designation.

Key Concepts from the Preface

  • Crown Ownership: In Canada, the Crown (federal and provincial governments) is the ultimate owner of all land.
  • Fee Simple Estate: This is the highest or largest bundle of rights and responsibilities that an individual can hold in land. It's the closest practical equivalent to absolute ownership.
  • Capitalized Value: The value of land is not just its current price, but the dollar amount invested today that is justified by all anticipated future benefits (e.g., rent, use, profit).
  • Mortgagor vs. Mortgagee: The **Borrower** is the mortgag*or* (the one who *grants* the mortgage). The **Lender** is the mortgag*ee* (the one who *receives* the mortgage).
  • Mortgage Broker Role: A mortgage broker acts as an intermediary who works *for the borrower* to find and negotiate suitable financing from various lenders.
  • Broker Compensation: Typically paid by the lender as a commission, calculated in "basis points" (bps). 100 bps = 1% of the loan amount.
  • Mortgage Insurance: *Required* for all high-ratio loans (where the loan is more than 80% of the property value). This insurance protects the *lender* against borrower default. Key insurers are CMHC, Sagen, and Canada Guaranty.
  • Residential vs. ICandI: The Industrial, Commercial and Investment (ICandI) market involves larger, less frequent, and more complex transactions and financing compared to the residential market.
  • Property Characteristics: Real property is unique because it is (1) **Immobile**, (2) **Durable** (improvements last a long time), and (3) subject to **Externalities** (its value is affected by surrounding properties and factors).
  • Key Organizations (BC):
    • BCFSA (BC Financial Services Authority): The statutory regulator. It administers the *Mortgage Brokers Act*.
    • Registrar of Mortgage Brokers: The specific regulatory body within BCFSA that oversees broker registration and discipline.
    • CMBA-BC (Canadian Mortgage Brokers Association - BC): A voluntary professional association that provides education, advocacy, and enforces a Code of Ethics for its members.

Common Exam Traps

  • Fee Simple vs. Absolute Ownership: Don't confuse the two. An individual can *never* have absolute ownership of land in Canada. They hold a fee simple *interest* in the land, which is subject to Crown rights.
  • Mortgagor vs. Mortgagee: This is a classic. Remember: Borrowor, Lendee. The "or" gives, the "ee" receives.
  • Who the Broker Works For: An exam might imply the broker works for the lender, especially since the lender pays them. This is false. The broker's primary duty is to the *borrower* who hired them.
  • Mortgage Insurance Purpose: Remember who the insurance protects. It is paid by the borrower, but it protects the *lender*.
  • BCFSA vs. CMBA-BC: Don't mix up the regulator with the professional association. BCFSA is the *law* (statutory, government body). CMBA-BC is the *club* (voluntary, industry body). BCFSA can fine you, suspend your license, and has legal authority. CMBA-BC can revoke your *membership*.

Chapter 1: Fundamentals of Law

This chapter provides the legal framework necessary to understand real property and contract law. It establishes that law in British Columbia comes from two main sources: **Statute Law** and **Common Law**.

Sources of Law

Statute Law is the body of law made by government. In Canada, power is divided:
  • Federal Government: Has power over areas like banking, bankruptcy, and criminal law.
  • Provincial Government: Has power over "property and civil rights," which is why it regulates real estate and mortgage brokerage.
  • Municipal Government: Gets its power from the province to pass bylaws (e.g., zoning).

Common Law is the body of law developed by judges through court decisions. It is based on the principle of *stare decisis* (Latin for "let the former decision stand"), also known as the doctrine of precedent. This means that a court is bound by the decisions of higher courts in cases with similar facts, which ensures consistency and predictability in the law.

Common Law vs. Equity

Historically, two separate court systems existed in England: the courts of Common Law and the courts of Equity (or Chancery).
  • The **Common Law** courts were rigid. Their *only* remedy for a breach of contract was **damages** (monetary compensation).
  • The courts of **Equity** were created to provide "fairness" where the strict application of common law was unjust. Equity developed unique remedies, including:
    • Specific Performance: A court order forcing a party to complete their end of the contract (e.g., forcing a seller to go through with the sale of a house).
    • Injunction: A court order stopping a party from doing a wrongful act.

Today, the systems are "fused." A BC Supreme Court judge can apply both. If there is a conflict between a common law rule and an equitable principle, **equity prevails**. This is a critical concept, especially in real estate, where a buyer might want the house itself (specific performance) rather than just money (damages).

The BC Court System (Civil)

If a dispute is not handled by an **Administrative Tribunal** (a specialized, non-court body like the Civil Resolution Tribunal or "CRT"), it goes through the court system:

  1. Provincial Court (Small Claims): The first level of trial court, for civil claims up to **$35,000**.
  2. BC Supreme Court: The main trial court for claims over $35,000 and for serious matters like foreclosures. It also hears appeals from the Small Claims Court.
  3. BC Court of Appeal: The highest court *in the province*. It does not re-hear the trial (i.e., no new witnesses or evidence). It only reviews the trial judge's decision to see if an error in *law* was made.
  4. Supreme Court of Canada: The final court for all of Canada. It is not an automatic right to appeal; parties must apply for "leave" (permission) to have their case heard.

The Civil Lawsuit Process

A typical lawsuit proceeds in distinct stages:

  • Pleadings: The formal written statements from each side. The **Plaintiff** (the one suing) files a **Notice of Civil Claim**. The **Defendant** (the one being sued) files a **Response to Civil Claim**.
  • Discovery: The pre-trial stage where each side "discovers" the other's case. This includes **Discovery of Documents** (exchanging all relevant files) and **Examination for Discovery** (questioning the other party under oath before a court reporter). Most cases are settled after this stage.
  • Trial: The case is argued before a judge. The plaintiff has the **burden of proof** and must prove their case on a **balance of probabilities** (i.e., their version of events is more likely than not, or 50% + 1).
  • Judgment: The judge's binding decision. The winning party is usually awarded **costs**, which is a *partial* reimbursement of their legal fees, paid by the loser.

Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)

To avoid the cost and time of a trial, parties often use ADR:
  • Mediation: A neutral third-party (the mediator) *facilitates* a negotiation to help the parties reach their own settlement. The mediator has no power to impose a decision.
  • Arbitration: A neutral third-party (the arbitrator) acts like a private judge. The arbitrator hears evidence from both sides and *makes a binding decision* that is enforceable by a court.

Enforcing a Judgment and Limitation Periods

If a defendant refuses to pay a judgment, the plaintiff can take steps to enforce it, such as a **Garnishing Order** (to seize wages or bank funds) or a **Writ of Execution** (to seize and sell assets). A plaintiff cannot wait forever to sue; the **Limitation Act** sets a time limit, which for most civil claims is 2 years from the date the claim was (or ought to have been) *discovered*.

Key Legal Concepts

  • Sources of Law:
    1. Common Law: Judge-made law based on precedent.
    2. Statute Law: Government-made law (e.g., the *Mortgage Brokers Act*).
  • Stare Decisis: The principle of precedent. It creates predictability and fairness in the law.
  • Common Law vs. Equity:
    • Common Law Remedy: Damages (monetary compensation).
    • Equitable Remedies: Specific Performance (forcing sale/action) and Injunction (stopping an action).
    • The Rule: If Common Law and Equity conflict, **Equity prevails**.
  • BC Court System (Civil):
    • Small Claims Court: Hears cases for claims up to $35,000.
    • BC Supreme Court: The main trial court with no monetary limit. Hears appeals from Small Claims.
    • BC Court of Appeal: Hears appeals from the BC Supreme Court. Does *not* re-hear evidence, only reviews matters of law.
    • Supreme Court of Canada: The final court of appeal. Parties must apply for "leave" (permission) to have their case heard.
  • Civil Trial Process:
    • Pleadings: Filing the Notice of Civil Claim (by plaintiff) and Response (by defendant).
    • Discovery: Parties exchange documents and question each other under oath (Examination for Discovery).
    • Trial: Evidence is presented before a judge.
    • Judgment: The judge's binding decision.
  • Limitation Period: A person must start a lawsuit within a specific time limit (e.g., 2 years for most civil claims) from the date the claim is "discovered."

Common Exam Traps

  • Equitable Remedies: Know the difference between Damages (money) and Specific Performance (action). An exam question might ask what remedy a buyer has if a seller backs out. Specific performance is a key answer.
  • Equity Prevails: A classic exam question. If a common law rule (e.g., "the contract is broken, pay damages") conflicts with an equitable principle (e.g., "it is unfair to only pay damages, you must complete the sale"), the equitable principle wins.
  • Small Claims Limit: Memorize the $35,000 limit.
  • Appellate vs. Trial Courts: An appellate court (Court of Appeal) does *not* call witnesses or re-hear the case. It only reviews the trial judge's application of the law.

Chapter 2: The Mortgage Brokers Act and Professional Ethics

This chapter is the regulatory core of the course, detailing the *Mortgage Brokers Act* (MBA), the legislation that governs the industry. The primary purpose of the Act is to **protect the public**. It achieves this by ensuring licensees are competent, setting standards of conduct, and establishing a regulator to enforce the rules.

The Regulator: BCFSA and the Registrar

The Act is administered by the **Registrar of Mortgage Brokers**. The Registrar and their office are part of the **BC Financial Services Authority (BCFSA)**, the independent Crown agency that regulates mortgage brokers, real estate licensees, credit unions, and more.

Who Must Be Registered?

The Act defines two types of registrants:

  • Mortgage Broker: The *business entity* (the company, partnership, or sole proprietorship). This is the firm that holds the brokerage licence.
  • Submortgage Broker: The *individual* (you) who is employed by, or a director/partner of, a mortgage broker. You perform the brokerage activities *on behalf of* the mortgage broker.
An individual must be registered as a submortgage broker if they engage in any mortgage broker activities, such as:
  • Lending money secured by mortgages (as a business).
  • Holding oneself out as a mortgage broker.
  • Arranging mortgages for others for a fee (exceeding $1,000/year).
  • Lending money on 10 or more mortgages in a year.

Operating or employing an unregistered person is a serious offence, carrying fines up to $100,000 (first offence) and/or imprisonment.

Exemptions from Registration

Crucially, not everyone who deals in mortgages must register. The Act provides specific exemptions for:
  • Banks, credit unions, and insurance companies.
  • Lawyers, *only if* the mortgage transaction is part of their normal legal practice.
  • A real estate licensee is **NOT** exempt. The *only* exception is if they are arranging a **vendor take-back mortgage** that is ancillary to a property sale they are already representing.
  • Unregistered assistants can perform administrative work but are strictly prohibited from *any* brokerage activity, including soliciting clients, providing mortgage advice, or taking applications. The registered broker is liable for their assistant's actions.

Powers of the Registrar

The Registrar has broad powers to enforce the Act:
  • Investigation: Can investigate any matter (based on a complaint or on their own initiative), enter a broker's office, inspect and seize records, and compel witnesses to give evidence.
  • Disciplinary Action: If a broker breaches the Act, acts prejudicially to the public interest, or is party to an unconscionable transaction, the Registrar can (after a hearing):
    • Suspend, cancel, or place conditions on a registration.
    • Issue a **cease and desist order**.
    • Order an **administrative penalty (fine) up to $50,000**.
  • "Fronting": The Registrar actively disciplines brokers who facilitate unregistered activity, known as "fronting," where a registered broker submits applications for an unregistered person.

Market Conduct and Disclosure

This is the most critical part of the chapter, detailing the rules of conduct, particularly around disclosure.

Disclosure under the BPCPA

The *Business Practices and Consumer Protection Act (BPCPA)* also applies to mortgage brokers. It requires brokers to provide borrowers (for personal, family, or household purposes) with a detailed **Disclosure Statement** two business days *before* the borrower is obligated. This statement must clearly show the full cost of credit, including the Annual Percentage Rate (APR).

Form 9: Disclosure to Lender/Investor

This form is a *statutory requirement* under the MBA.
  • To Whom: It must be given to any lender or investor who is *not* a "sophisticated person" (e.g., a private individual, but not a bank).
  • When: It must be provided *before* the lender advances any funds.
  • Purpose: To ensure the private lender understands *all* the risks of the investment. It discloses details about the borrower, the property, the mortgage terms, all prior encumbrances (other mortgages), and the loan-to-value ratio.

Form 10: Conflict of Interest Disclosure

This is arguably the most important disclosure form for a broker.
  • To Whom: It must be given to **ALL** parties, specifically the **borrower AND the lender**.
  • Purpose: To disclose any *direct or indirect interest* the broker, sub-broker, or any related party has in the transaction. This includes *all* forms of compensation:
    • Commissions from the lender (e.g., 100 bps).
    • Volume bonuses or rewards points.
    • Trailer fees for renewal.
    • Any fees paid by the borrower.
    • Any personal or business relationship with any party.
  • The Standard: The disclosure must be **clear, comprehensive, and meaningful**, allowing the parties to understand the broker's potential conflicts and give their informed consent.

Other Rules of Conduct

  • Deceptive/Unconscionable Acts (BPCPA): Brokers are prohibited from misleading consumers or taking advantage of a borrower's vulnerability (e.g., age, ignorance, language barrier).
  • Misleading Advertising: All advertising must be truthful and not deceptive. It must also clearly state the broker's registered name.

Professional Ethics

The chapter concludes by distinguishing legal duties from ethical ones.
  • Law (The MBA): Is mandatory and enforced by a government regulator (BCFSA). The consequences for a breach are legal (fines, suspension).
  • **Ethics (Code of Ethics):** Are moral principles and standards of conduct enforced by a *voluntary* professional association (like **CMBA-BC**). The consequences for a breach are professional (e.g., reprimand, expulsion from the *association*).

Ethical duties include the duty to uphold the law, a duty of competence to the client, a duty of honesty, a fiduciary duty (where applicable), and a duty of confidentiality.

Key Regulatory Concepts

  • Governing Body: The *Mortgage Brokers Act* is the law. It is enforced by the **Registrar of Mortgage Brokers**, which is part of **BCFSA**.
  • Broker vs. Sub-broker: The "Mortgage Broker" is the firm/company. The "Submortgage Broker" is the individual. You are studying to become a submortgage broker.
  • Mandatory Registration: You *must* be registered as a submortgage broker and employed by a registered mortgage broker to trade in mortgages.
  • Key Exemptions: Those *not* required to register include:
    • Banks, Credit Unions, and Insurance Companies.
    • Lawyers, *only if* the mortgage is part of their normal legal practice.
    • A real estate licensee is **NOT** exempt, *except* when arranging a vendor take-back mortgage that is ancillary to a sale they are representing.
  • Registrar's Powers: The Registrar can investigate, demand documents, freeze accounts, suspend or cancel registration, and issue fines up to $50,000 for misconduct.
  • Form 9 - Lender Disclosure: Provided **TO THE LENDER** (if not a "sophisticated person" like a bank). Discloses property details, borrower info, and investment risks. Must be provided *before* funds are advanced.
  • Form 10 - Conflict of Interest: Provided **TO BOTH BORROWER AND LENDER**. Discloses *all* compensation (commissions, bonuses, finder's fees, etc.) the broker or sub-broker will receive.
  • "Fronting": The illegal practice of a registered broker submitting a mortgage application on behalf of an *unregistered* individual.
  • Ethics vs. Law:
    • Law (MBA): Enforced by BCFSA/Registrar. Breach leads to legal penalties (fines, suspension).
    • Ethics (Code of Ethics): Enforced by voluntary associations (e.g., CMBA-BC). Breach leads to professional sanction (e.g., membership revocation).

Principles of Conflict of Interest Disclosure (Form 10)

1. Clear and Understandable

Interests must be described in simple language, free of industry jargon.

2. Comprehensive and Accurate

Sufficient detail (qualitative and quantitative) must be provided to fully describe the interest.

3. Meaningful to Users

Users must be able to understand the interest and make an informed decision.

Common Exam Traps

  • Broker vs. Sub-broker: You will be tested on this. You are the *sub-broker*. Your company is the *broker*.
  • Form 9 vs. Form 10: This is a critical distinction.
    • Form 9 Lender ONLY. (Think: "Number 9 is for the investor's dime").
    • Form 10 Borrower AND Lender. (Think: "Form 10 tells *all* of them").
  • Exemptions: Know them. A bank employee doesn't need to register. A lawyer arranging a mortgage for a client as part of a home purchase doesn't. A real estate agent *does* need to register, unless it's just a vendor take-back.
  • BPCPA: The *Business Practices and Consumer Protection Act* also applies to mortgage brokers, especially regarding deceptive acts and disclosure.
  • Designated Individual (DI): This is the registered submortgage broker (usually an owner or director) who is responsible for supervising the brokerage and its sub-brokers.

Chapter 3: What the Purchaser Buys: Estates and Interests in Land

This chapter details what a person actually "buys" when they purchase real estate. It moves from the theoretical (the Doctrine of Estates) to the practical (fixtures vs. chattels, and co-ownership).

The Doctrine of Estates

The chapter begins by reinforcing the concept that the Crown is the absolute owner of all land. Therefore, a purchaser does not buy the land itself, but rather an **estate** in the land. An estate is a "bundle of rights" that defines the owner's rights and interests, particularly the *duration* for which they can hold the land. The two main categories of estates are Freehold Estates (which have an uncertain duration) and Leasehold Estates (which have a defined duration).

Freehold Estates

There are two main types of freehold estates discussed:

  1. Fee Simple Estate: This is the highest form of ownership and what we commonly think of as "owning" property. It is the largest bundle of rights possible. The holder (owner) has the right to use, sell, and lease the property, and it is inheritable (it can be left to someone in a will). Its duration is uncertain because it can, in theory, last forever, being passed down through generations.
  2. Life Estate: This estate's duration is limited to the lifetime of a specific person, known as the **life tenant**. The life tenant has the right to possess and use the property (and collect any rent/profits) during their lifetime. However, they cannot sell or will the fee simple estate. When the life tenant dies, the estate automatically goes to a designated "remainderman" or reverts to the original grantor (a "reversioner").
    • Estate Pur Autre Vie: A special type of life estate where the duration is measured by the life of a *different* person (e.g., "I grant the property to my brother, for as long as our mother lives").
    • The Doctrine of Waste: This is a critical limitation on life tenants. They cannot do anything to devalue the property for the future remainderman. The chapter details four types of waste: voluntary waste (intentional damage, like demolishing a garage), permissive waste (letting the property fall into disrepair), ameliorating waste (making "improvements" that may not be wanted by the remainderman), and equitable waste (severe, malicious damage).

From a mortgage broker's perspective, a life estate is very difficult to get a mortgage on because the lender's security (the estate) disappears when the life tenant dies.

Leasehold Estate

This is the second main category of estate. Unlike a freehold estate, its duration is *certain* and specified for a fixed period (e.g., a one-year apartment lease or a 99-year ground lease). The person who owns the property and grants the lease is the *lessor* (landlord), and the person who receives the lease is the *lessee* (tenant).

Interests in Land Less Than Estates

These are rights in land that are *not* estates because they do not grant the right of possession. They are "charges" that are registered on the property's title.

  • Easement: This is a right to *use* a part of another person's land for a specific purpose. It requires two properties: a **dominant tenement** (the property that *benefits* from the easement) and a **servient tenement** (the property that is *burdened* by the use). A classic example is a right-of-way allowing one property owner to drive over a portion of their neighbour's land to reach their own.
  • Restrictive Covenant: This is a *restriction* on what an owner can do with their own land. It is a negative promise (e.g., "you shall *not* build a structure over two stories," "you shall *not* paint your fence blue"). It "runs with the land," meaning it binds all future owners of the property.
  • Building Scheme: A specific set of restrictive covenants registered against an entire subdivision by the developer to ensure uniformity (e.g., all homes must be brick, have a minimum square footage, etc.).
  • Licence: This is *NOT* an interest in land. It is merely personal permission to enter someone's property (e.g., a movie ticket, an invitation to a party). It cannot be registered on title, does not "run with the land," and can be revoked by the owner.

The Extent of "Ownership"

This section defines the physical limits of an owner's rights.

  • Fixtures vs. Chattels: This is a critical legal distinction.
    • A **Chattel** is personal, movable property (e.g., a sofa, table lamp).
    • A **Fixture** is a chattel that has been attached to the property in such a way that it is now legally considered part of the land (e.g., a furnace, a built-in cabinet). Fixtures are *included* in the sale of the property unless specifically excluded.
    • The Test: To tell the difference, the court asks: (1) What was the *degree* of attachment? (Slightly attached items are presumed to be fixtures). (2) What was the *purpose* of the attachment? Was it attached to better use the *item* (making it a chattel) or to better use the *land/building* (making it a fixture)? The purpose is the more important part of the test.
  • Airspace and Subsurface Rights: An owner owns the airspace above their land to the extent they can effectively use it. The Crown, however, reserves the rights to most subsurface resources like minerals, oil, and natural gas.
  • Water Rights: An owner does *not* own the water that flows through their property. Under the *Water Sustainability Act*, they must obtain a licence to use it.

Co-Ownership of Land

This describes how two or more people can own a property together.

  • Joint Tenancy (JT): This form of ownership has one defining feature: the **Right of Survivorship**. When one joint tenant dies, their share *automatically* goes to the surviving joint tenant(s). Their share *cannot* be passed to someone else in a will. To create a JT, the "four unities" must be present: Unity of Time, Title, Interest, and Possession. This is common for married couples.
  • Tenancy in Common (TIC): This is the default form of co-ownership, especially for business partners. The *only* unity required is Possession. Each owner holds a separate, distinct share (e.g., 60% and 40%). There is **NO right of survivorship**. Each owner *can* sell their share or leave it to someone in their will.

Torts Relating to Land

The chapter concludes by mentioning civil wrongs related to land, including:
  • Trespass: Entering someone's land without permission.
  • Nuisance: Unreasonably interfering with a neighbour's use and enjoyment of their property (e.g., with loud noises, bad smells).
  • Occupier's Liability: The *Occupier's Liability Act* states that an occupier (owner or tenant) owes a duty of care to ensure that any person on their property is reasonably safe.

Key Legal Concepts

  • Fee Simple Estate: The largest "bundle of rights" a person can own. This is what you buy and sell.
  • Life Estate: An estate that lasts only for the lifetime of a specific person (the "life tenant").
  • Leasehold Estate: An estate for a fixed, predetermined duration (a lease).
  • Easement: The right to *use* another's land for a specific purpose (e.g., a driveway). It *is* an interest in land.
  • Restrictive Covenant: A rule that *restricts* the use of a property (e.g., "no structures over 2 stories"). It *is* an interest in land.
  • Licence: Personal *permission* to be on land (e.g., a hockey ticket). It is **NOT** an interest in land.
  • Fixtures vs. Chattels: Fixtures (like a furnace) are attached and part of the real estate. Chattels (like a sofa) are personal property. The test is the *purpose* of the attachment.
  • Joint Tenancy (JT): The key feature is the **Right of Survivorship**. When one owner dies, their share automatically goes to the other owner(s). It cannot be willed.
  • Tenancy in Common (TIC): The key feature is **No Right of Survivorship**. Each owner has a separate share that *can* be sold or willed to anyone. This is the default form of co-ownership.
  • Doctrine of Waste: The rule that a life tenant cannot damage or devalue the property to the detriment of the future "remainderman."

Common Exam Traps

  • Joint Tenancy vs. Tenancy in Common: This is the *most common* trap from this chapter. You *must* know the difference. The key is the **Right of Survivorship**. If a question involves one owner wanting to leave their share to their children, they MUST be Tenancy in Common.
  • Easement vs. Licence: An exam may describe a scenario and ask if it's an easement or a licence. An easement is a registered right that "runs with the land." A licence is just personal permission and can be revoked.
  • Fixtures: A question will almost certainly test this. A heavy, unattached statue is a chattel. A small, bolted-on doorbell is a fixture. Remember, the *purpose* of the attachment (was it intended to be permanent and improve the building?) is more important than the *degree* of attachment (how many bolts it has).
  • Life Estates and Mortgages: A lender is extremely unlikely to grant a mortgage secured *only* by a Life Estate, because their security vanishes when the life tenant dies.
